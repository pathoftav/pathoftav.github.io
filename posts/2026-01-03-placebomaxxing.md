# You should be Placebomaxxing


## The Elixir

Imagine that you're spending the week at my house when you come down with a bad case of food poisoning because you undercooked our dinner last night. While I managed to escape unscathed, you lay on the couch groaning from excruciating stomach pain, terrible nausea that makes your head spin, and a deep fear that I won't ever trust your cooking again.

You see me approach, steaming cup in hand, and tell you to sit up for a moment. In a hushed tone I gently say to you —

_"Several years ago, I spent three months as a student of a shaman up in the Beartooth Mountains of Montana where he taught me how to be a medicine man among various other things. In my hands is a special tea that I've brewed for you; a combination of moon lily, hollow reed, and mimosa root — all of which I grow out back in my garden. It's an ancient herbal remedy for pain and nausea that has been passed down and perfected over centuries. Drink this, and you will immediately start feeling better."_

You sip on the bitter elixir, and within moments feel a calming ease wash over your body. Most of your pain has vanished and your nausea is fading into a background noise that you can mostly ignore. You're grateful that my ancient herbal knowledge and time in the mountains could help soothe your discomfort in your hour of self-inflicted need.

The thing is, everything that I just said was bullshit. I went out to my backyard, pulled up some dandelions, mashed them into a paste, and boiled it into tea. There were exactly zero medicinal molecules in the "magical elixir" that you drank.

You've just become a victim of the placebo effect.


## Power of Placebo

There is a reason that in every single study the control group isn't —

_"people who didn't take the medicine"_

but rather,

_"people who **thought** they took the medicine"_
